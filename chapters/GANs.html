Probability Density (\(p(x)\)) v/s Probability Distribution (\(\mathbb{P}(x)\))

Discrete Variable:

\[P(X = x) = p(x) \quad \text{(Probability Mass Function)}\] \[\mathbb{P}(X \in A) = \sum_{x \in A} p(x)\]

Continuous Variable:

No Probability Mass Function exists for continous variables (Cant find probability at a single point), but

\[\mathbb{P}(X \in A) = \int_A p(x) \, dx\]

i.i.d (Independent and Identically Distributed)

Identical:

If 2 R.V's is drawn from the same distribution, then it is said to be identical.

Independent:

If the outcome of one R.V does not affect the outcome of another R.V, then they are said to be independent.

Consider 2 random variables \(X_1, X_2\) that are independent.

Then:

\[P(X_1 = x_1 | X_2 = x_2) = P(X_1 = x_1)\]
Note: Whenever the term i.i.d is used, Law of Large Numbers is activated for that R.V. \[ \text{LLN: } \quad \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \xrightarrow{P} \mathbb{E}[x] \quad \text{as } n \to \infty \]

Convex Conjugate

Consider the graph given below.

Here, \( f(u) \) is some function(convex or non-convex) which we're interested in finding the convex conjugate of.

and now we pick an arbitrary slope t, and line l(u) = tu + c

to say that the line is convex conjugate, it must satisfy:

\[ l(u) \leq f(u) \quad \text{for all } u \] \[ tu + c \leq f(u) \quad \text{for all } u \] \[ c \leq f(u) - tu \quad \text{for all } u \]

now, our job is to minimize c

\[ c = \inf_{u} \left\{ f(u) - tu \right\} \]

or,

\[ -c = \sup_{u} \left\{ tu - f(u) \right\} \]

We can write convex conjugate as a function of t:

\[ f^*(t) = \sup_{u} \left\{ tu - f(u) \right\} \]
Note: We've used the supremum (sup) instead of maximum (max) because the maximum may not be attained if \( f \) is not continuous or does not have a maximum value.

Ex: Consider the function \( f(x) \) mapped in the range \([0, 1)\) and \( f(x) = 0 \) for all \( x \in [0, 1) \).

\[max_{x \in [0, 1)} \{ f(x) \} = undefined\] \[sup_{x \in [0, 1)} \{ f(x) \} = 1\]

Generative Adversarial Networks

Let's derive the convex conjugate of \( f(u) = u \log u - (u+1)\log(u+1) \)

We want to find its convex conjugate, defined as: \[ f^*(t) = \sup_{u > 0} \left\{ tu - u \log u + (u+1)\log(u+1) \right\} \] Step 1: Find the critical point
Take the derivative of the expression inside the supremum with respect to u and set it to 0: \[ \frac{d}{du} \left( tu - u \log u + (u+1)\log(u+1) \right) = t - (\log u + 1) + (\log(u+1) + 1) = 0 \] Simplifying this, we get the relationship between t and u at the optimum: \[ t = \log u - \log(u+1) \] (Note: Since \( u > 0 \), the ratio \( \frac{u}{u+1} < 1 \), which implies \( t < 0 \).)

Step 2: Simplify the objective function
Instead of solving for u right away, substitute \( t = \log u - \log(u+1) \) directly into the original supremum expression: \[ f^*(t) = u(\log u - \log(u+1)) - u \log u + (u+1)\log(u+1) \] Expand the terms: \[ f^*(t) = u \log u - u \log(u+1) - u \log u + u \log(u+1) + \log(u+1) \] Notice how beautifully almost everything cancels out, leaving just: \[ f^*(t) = \log(u+1) \] Step 3: Solve for u+1 in terms of t
Now we just need to express \( \log(u+1) \) in terms of t. Using our relationship from Step 1: \[ t = \log\left(\frac{u}{u+1}\right) \] \[ e^t = \frac{u}{u+1} \] We can rewrite the right side as: \[ e^t = 1 - \frac{1}{u+1} \] Rearranging to solve for u+1: \[ \frac{1}{u+1} = 1 - e^t \implies u+1 = \frac{1}{1 - e^t} \] Step 4: Final Substitution
Substitute u+1 back into our simplified expression for \( f^*(t) \): \[ f^*(t) = \log\left(\frac{1}{1 - e^t}\right) \] Using the property of logarithms \( \log(1/x) = -\log(x) \), we get the final answer: \[ \boxed{ f^*(t) = -\log(1 - e^t) } \qquad \text{for } t < 0 \]
